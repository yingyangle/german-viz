What is German?

German is a West Germanic language of the Indo-European language family. This means that it's distantly related to other Indo-European languages like Spanish, Russian, Greek, and even Hindi, while it's more closely related to other Germanic languages such as Swedish, Dutch, and English. Today, German is spoken primarily in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, with significant additional populations in South Tyrol (Italy), Belgium, and Liechtenstein. There are about 85 million native speakers of German and 15 million non-native speakers of German.


The Influence of German

If you take a look at German vocabulary, it appears that German actually has a lot in common with English. Many words in English such as "house" sound very much like their German equivalent "Haus". These pairs of words are called cognates, meaning the words share the same root origin. Take a look at some common cognates between English and German below.

The reason we see all these similarities is because English is a Germanic language just like German. When the Anglo-Saxon tribes migrated to Britain from northern Germany and the Netherlands around the mid 5th to 7th centuries AD, they brought with them their Germanic language which would eventually become English. English then received a lot of influence from other languages such as French, Latin, and Greek in the centuries following, but much of our English grammar is still recognizably Germanic and about 26% of our vocabulary is Germanic in origin. We can still see the traces of our Germanic origins in many of the words we use today.

We also have a lot of loanwords in English taken directly from German vocabulary. German has many interesting words to express very specific and relatable concepts that we don't have words for in English.
