Why learn German?

  • With Germany being one of the largest European trading partners with the US, learning German can strengthen a business career by being able to communicate directly with German business partners.
  • German is easy for English speakers to learn because both languages belong to the Germanic branch of the Indo-European language family. As can be seen in the loanwords visualization, some English words were borrowed from German.
  • German is the most widely spoken language in Europe as Germany not only has the highest population in Europe, it is the official language of Austria, Switzerland, and several other European countries.

What is German?

German is a West Germanic language of the Indo-European language family. This means that it's distantly related to other Indo-European languages like Spanish, Russian, Greek, and even Hindi, while it's more closely related to other Germanic languages such as Swedish, Dutch, and English. Today, German is spoken primarily in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, with significant additional populations in South Tyrol (Italy), Belgium, and Liechtenstein.

The reason we see all these similarities is because English is a Germanic language just like German. When the Germanic Anglo-Saxon tribes migrated to Britain from northern Germany around the mid 5th to 7th centuries AD, they brought with them their Germanic language which would eventually become English. English then received a lot of influence from other languages such as French, Latin, and Greek in the centuries to come, but much of our English grammar is still recognizably Germanic and about 26% of our vocabulary is Germanic in origin. We can still see traces of our Germanic origins in many of the words we use today.

German Grammar

While the English and German may be historically related, there are many differences in the grammar of German that may pose difficulties for students of German. The Foreign Language Institute provides an overview categorizing the difficulty of learning foreign languages for native English speakers, and German is the only language in the "Category II" group, meaning it requires approximately 750 hours of study. This makes German easier to learn than languages like Russian and Indonesian, but harder than languages like French and Italian.

But what exactly makes German so difficult? Let's take a look at some of the major aspects of German grammar that differ from English.

Word Order

In English, our word order is relatively simple but very strict, meaning that if you change the order of the words, it changes the meaning of sentence. Other languages with a very strict word order include languages like Mandarin or Cantonese.

" The dog bit the mailman."

"The mailman bit the dog."

You might also be familiar with languages where word order is more free, meaning that words can move around in the sentence, often for emphasis. These languages are usually highly inflected, which means that they have a lot of noun endings to indicate the role of the noun in the sentence (e.g. subject vs. object). Languages that have relatively free word order include Latin, Greek, Russian, Korean, and Japanese.

In German, word order is also somewhat strict, but in a very different way. While English word order follows the pattern of Subject + Verb + Object, the biggest rule for German word order is that the main verb must come second in the sentence. The other parts of the sentence can move around more freely, but the verb always has to be cecond.

For example, look at the word order in the following sentences. The main verb ("write") always stays in second position, while the other parts of the sentence such as the subject ("I"), direct object ("stories") and prepositional phrases ("for my friends") can move around in the sentence. Despite the different word order, each of thesee sentences means "I write stories for my friends."

Ich schreibe Geschichten für meine Freunde
I write stories for my friends

Geschichten schreibe ich für meine Freunde
stories write I for my friends

Für meine Freund schreibe ich Geschichten
for my friends write I stories

Furthermore, the word order gets more complicated when you introduce things like complementary infinitives, which the must go at the end of the sentence, and subordinate clauses, which send the verb from second position to the end of the clause.

Ich möchte in meinem Bett schlafen
I want in my bed to sleep
"I want to sleep in my bed."
(complementary infintive = "to sleep")

Ich sagte ihr, dass ich große weiche Kissen mag
I told her that I big soft pillows like
"I told her that I like big soft pillows ."
(subordinate clause in blue)
